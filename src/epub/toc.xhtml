  5. Selections from the Principles of Philosophy of Descartes
    1. Letter of the Author to the French Translator of the Principles of Philosophy Serving for a Preface
    2. To the Most Serene Princess, Elizabeth, Eldest Daughter of Frederick, King of Bohemia, Count Palatine, and Elector of the Sacred Roman Empire
    3. Part I: Of the Principles of Human Knowledge
      1. I: That in Order to Seek Truth, It Is Necessary Once in the Course of Our Life, to Doubt, as Far as Possible, of All Things
      2. II: That We Ought Also to Consider as False All That Is Doubtful
      3. III: That We Ought Not Meanwhile to Make Use of Doubt in the Conduct of Life
      4. IV: Why We May Doubt of Sensible Things
      5. V: Why We May Also Doubt of Mathematical Demonstrations
      6. VI: That We Possess a Free Will, by Which We Can Withhold Our Assent from What Is Doubtful, and Thus Avoid Error
      7. VII: That We Cannot Doubt of Our Existence While We Doubt, and That This Is the First Knowledge We Acquire When We Philosophize in Order
      8. VIII: That We Hence Discover the Distinction Between the Mind and the Body, or Between a Thinking and Corporeal Thing
      9. IX: What Thought (Cogitatio) Is
      10. X: That the Notions Which Are Simplest and Self-Evident, Are Obscured by Logical Definitions; and That Such Are Not to Be Reckoned Among the Cognitions Acquired by Study, [But as Born with Us]
      11. XI: How We Can Know Our Mind More Clearly Than Our Body
      12. XII: How It Happens That Every One Does Not Come Equally to Know This
      13. XIII: In What Sense the Knowledge of Other Things Depends Upon the Knowledge of God
      14. XIV: That We May Validly Infer the Existence of God from Necessary Existence Being Comprised in the Concept We Have of Him
      15. XV: That Necessary Existence Is Not in the Same Way Comprised in the Notions Which We Have of Other Things, but Merely Contingent Existence
      16. XVI: That Prejudices Hinder Many from Clearly Knowing the Necessity of the Existence of God
      17. XVII: That the Greater Objective (Representative) Perfection There Is in Our Idea of a Thing, the Greater Also Must Be the Perfection of Its Cause
      18. XVIII: That the Existence of God May Be Again Inferred from the Above
      19. XIX: That, Although We May Not Comprehend the Nature of God, There Is Yet Nothing Which We Know So Clearly as His Perfections
      20. XX: That We Are Not the Cause of Ourselves, but That This Is God, and Consequently That There Is a God
      21. XXI: That the Duration Alone of Our Life Is Sufficient to Demonstrate the Existence of God
      22. XXII: That in Knowing the Existence of God, in the Manner Here Explained, We Likewise Know All His Attributes, as Far as They Can Be Known by the Natural Light Alone
      23. XXIII: That God Is Not Corporeal, and Does Not Perceive by Means of Senses as We Do, or Will the Evil of Sin
      24. XXIV: That in Passing from the Knowledge of God to the Knowledge of the Creatures, It Is Necessary to Remember That Our Understanding Is Finite, and the Power of God Infinite
      25. XXV: That We Must Believe All That God Has Revealed, Although It May Surpass the Reach of Our Faculties
      26. XXVI: That It Is Not Needful to Enter Into Disputes Regarding the Infinite, but Merely to Hold All That in Which We Can Find No Limits as Indefinite, Such as the Extension of the World, the Divisibility of the Parts of Matter, the Number of the Stars, etc.
      27. XXVII: What Difference There Is Between the Indefinite and the Infinite
      28. XXVIII: That We Must Examine, Not the Final, but the Efficient, Causes of Created Things
      29. XXIX: That God Is Not the Cause of Our Errors
      30. XXX: That Consequently All Which We Clearly Perceive Is True, and That We Are Thus Delivered from the Doubts Above Proposed
      31. XXXI: That Our Errors Are, in Respect of God, Merely Negations, But, in Respect of Ourselves, Privations
      32. XXXII: That There Are Only Two Modes of Thinking in Us, Viz., the Perception of the Understanding and the Action of the Will
      33. XXXIII: That We Never Err Unless When We Judge of Something Which We Do Not Sufficiently Apprehend
      34. XXXIV: That the Will as Well as the Understanding Is Required for Judging
      35. XXXV: That the Will Is of Greater Extension Than the Understanding, and Is Thus the Source of Our Errors
      36. XXXVI: That Our Errors Cannot Be Imputed to God
      37. XXXVII: That the Chief Perfection of Man Is His Being Able to Act Freely or by Will, and That It Is This Which Renders Him Worthy of Praise or Blame
      38. XXXVIII: That Error Is a Defect in Our Mode of Acting, Not in Our Nature; and That the Faults of Their Subjects May Be Frequently Attributed to Other Masters, but Never to God
      39. XXXIX: That the Liberty of Our Will Is Self-Evident
      40. XL: That It Is Likewise Certain That God Has Foreordained All Things
      41. XLI: How the Freedom of Our Will May Be Reconciled with the Divine Preordination
      42. XLII: How, Although We Never Will to Err, It Is Nevertheless by Our Will That We Do Err
      43. XLIII: That We Shall Never Err if We Give Our Assent Only to What We Clearly and Distinctly Perceive
      44. XLIV: That We Uniformly Judge Improperly When We Assent to What We Do Not Clearly Perceive, Although Our Judgment May Chance to Be True; and That It Is Frequently Our Memory Which Deceives Us by Leading Us to Believe That Certain Things Were Formerly Sufficiently Understood by Us
      45. XLV: What Constitutes Clear and Distinct Perception
      46. XLVI: It Is Shown, from the Example of Pain, That a Perception May Be Clear Without Being Distinct, but That It Cannot Be Distinct Unless It Is Clear
      47. XLVII: That, to Correct the Prejudices of Our Early Years, We Must Consider What Is Clear in Each of Our Simple Notions
      48. XLVIII: That All the Objects of Our Knowledge Are to Be Regarded Either (1) as Things or the Affections of Things: Or (2) as Eternal Truths; with the Enumeration of Things
      49. XLIX: That the Eternal Truths Cannot Be Thus Enumerated, but That This Is Not Necessary
      50. L: That These Truths Are Clearly Perceived, but Not Equally by All Men, on Account of Prejudices
      51. LI: What Substance Is, and That the Term Is Not Applicable to God and the Creatures in the Same Sense
      52. LII: That the Term Is Applicable Univocally to the Mind and the Body, and How Substance Itself Is Known
      53. LIII: That of Every Substance There Is One Principal Attribute, as Thinking of the Mind, Extension of the Body
      54. LIV: How We May Have Clear and Distinct Notions of the Substance Which Thinks, of That Which Is Corporeal, and of God
      55. LV: How Duration, Order, and Number May Be Also Distinctly Conceived
      56. LVI: What Are Modes, Qualities, Attributes
      57. LVII: That Some Attributes Exist in the Things to Which They Are Attributed, and Others Only in Our Thought; and What Duration and Time Are
      58. LVIII: That Number and All Universals Are Only Modes of Thought
      59. LIX: How Universals Are Formed; and What Are the Five Common, Viz., Genus, Species, Difference, Property, and Accident
      60. LX: Of Distinctions; and First of the Real
      61. LXI: Of the Modal Distinction
      62. LXII: Of the Distinction of Reason (Logical Distinction)
      63. LXIII: How Thought and Extension May Be Distinctly Known, as Constituting, the One the Nature of Mind, the Other That of Body
      64. LXIV: How These May Likewise Be Distinctly Conceived as Modes of Substance
      65. LXV: How We May Likewise Know Their Modes
      66. LXVI: How Our Sensations, Affections, and Appetites May Be Clearly Known, Although We Are Frequently Wrong in Our Judgments Regarding Them
      67. LXVII: That We Are Frequently Deceived in Our Judgments Regarding Pain Itself
      68. LXVIII: How in These Things What We Clearly Conceive Is to Be Distinguished from That in Which We May Be Deceived
      69. LXIX: That Magnitude, Figure, etc., Are Known Far Differently from Colour, Pain, etc.
      70. LXX: That We May Judge of Sensible Things in Two Ways, by the One of Which We Avoid Error, by the Other Fall Into It
      71. LXXI: That the Chief Cause of Our Errors Is to Be Found in the Prejudices of Our Childhood
      72. LXXII: That the Second Cause of Our Errors Is That We Cannot Forget These Prejudices
      73. LXXIII: The Third Cause Is, That We Become Fatigued by Attending to Those Objects Which Are Not Present to the Senses; and That We Are Thus Accustomed to Judge of These Not from Present Perception but from Preconceived Opinion
      74. LXXIV: The Fourth Source of Our Errors Is, That We Attach Our Thoughts to Words Which Do Not Express Them with Accuracy
      75. LXXV: Summary of What Must Be Observed in Order to Philosophize Correctly
      76. LXXVI: That We Ought to Prefer the Divine Authority to Our Perception: But That, Apart from Things Revealed, We Ought to Assent to Nothing That We Do Not Clearly Apprehend
    4. Part II: Of the Principles of Material Things
      1. I: The Grounds on Which the Existence of Material Things May Be Known with Certainty
      2. II: How We Likewise Know That the Human Body Is Closely Connected with the Mind
      3. III: That the Perceptions of the Senses Do Not Teach Us What Is in Reality in Things, but What Is Beneficial of Hurtful to the Composite Whole of Mind and Body
      4. IV: That the Nature of Body Consists Not in Weight, Hardness, Colour and the Like, but in Extension Alone
      5. V: That the Truth Regarding the Nature of Body Is Obscured by the Opinions Respecting Rarefaction and a Vacuum with Which We Are Preoccupied
      6. VI: In What Way Rarefaction Takes Place
      7. VII: That Rarefaction Cannot Be Intelligibly Explained Unless in the Way Here Proposed
      8. VIII: That Quantity and Number Differ Only in Thought (Ratione) from That Which Has Quantity and Is Numbered
      9. IX: That Corporeal Substance, When Distinguished from Its Quantity, Is Confusedly Conceived as Something Incorporeal
      10. X: What Space or Internal Place Is
      11. XI: How Space Is Not in Reality Different from Corporeal Substance
      12. XII: How Space Differs from Body in Our Mode of Conceiving It
      13. XIII: What External Place Is
      14. XIV: Wherein Place and Space Differ
      15. XV: How External Place Is Rightly Taken for the Superficies of the Surrounding Body
      16. XVI: That a Vacuum or Space in Which There Is Absolutely No Body Is Repugnant to Reason
      17. XVII: That a Vacuum in the Ordinary Use of the Term Does Not Exclude All Body
      18. XVIII: How the Prejudice of an Absolute Vacuum Is to Be Corrected
      19. XIX: That This Confirms What Was Said of Rarefaction
      20. XX: That from This the Nonexistence of Atoms May Likewise Be Demonstrated
      21. XXI: It Is Thus Also Demonstrated That the Extension of the World Is Indefinite
      22. XXII: It Also Follows That the Matter of the Heavens and Earth Is the Same, and That There Cannot Be a Plurality of Worlds
      23. XXIII: That All the Variety of Matter, or the Diversity of Its Forms, Depends on Motion
      24. XXIV: What Motion Is, Taking the Term in Its Common Use
      25. XXV: What Motion Is Properly So Called
    5. Part III: Of the Visible World
      1. I: That We Cannot Think Too Highly of the Works of God
      2. II: That We Ought to Beware Lest, in Our Presumption, We Imagine That the Ends Which God Proposed to Himself in the Creation of the World Are Understood by Us
      3. III: In What Sense It May Be Said That All Things Were Created for the Sake of Man
    6. Part IV: Of the Earth
      1. CLXXXVIII: Of What Is to Be Borrowed from Disquisitions on Animals and Man to Advance the Knowledge of Material Objects
      2. CLXXXIX: What Perception (Sensus) Is, and How We Perceive
      3. CXC: Of the Distinction of the Senses; and, First, of the Internal, That Is, of the Affections of the Mind (Passions), and the Natural Appetites
      4. CXCI: Of the External Senses; and First of Touch
      5. CXCII: Of Taste
      6. CXCIII: Of Smell
      7. CXCIV: Of Hearing
      8. CXCV: Of Sight
      9. CXCVI: That the Soul Perceives Only in So Far as It Is in the Brain
      10. CXCVII: That the Nature of the Mind Is Such That from the Motion Alone of Body the Various Sensations Can Be Excited in It
      11. CXCVIII: That by Our Senses We Know Nothing of External Objects Beyond Their Figure [Or Situation], Magnitude, and Motion
      12. CXCIX: That There Is No Phenomenon of Nature Whose Explanation Has Been Omitted in This Treatise
      13. CC: That This Treatise Contains No Principles Which Are Not Universally Received; and That This Philosophy Is Not New, but of All Others the Most Ancient and Common
      14. CCI: That Sensible Bodies Are Composed of Insensible Particles
      15. CCII: That the Philosophy of Democritus Is Not Less Different from Ours Than from the Common
      16. CCIII: How We May Arrive at the Knowledge of the Figures, [Magnitudes], and Motions of the Insensible Particles of Bodies
      17. CCIV: That, Touching the Things Which Our Senses Do Not Perceive, It Is Sufficient to Explain How They Can Be, [and That This Is All That Aristotle Has Essayed]
      18. CCV: That Nevertheless There Is a Moral Certainty That All the Things of This World Are Such as Has Been Here Shown They May Be
      19. CCVI: That We Possess Even More Than a Moral Certainty of It
      20. CCVII: That, However, I Submit All My Opinions to the Authority of the Church
